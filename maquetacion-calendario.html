GREEK VASE EXIBITION POSTER

EXIBITION POSTER

For the National Archaeological Museum (MAN) in Madrid

This poster was created as part of a university assignment for the subject Art History I. The task was to design a promotional poster for a fictional exhibition at the National Archaeological Museum (MAN) focused on ancient Greek vases. In addition to designing the layout and overall aesthetic of the poster, we were also required to illustrate the vase that would be featured in the exhibition. These vases had to include iconography representative of the types of narratives historically depicted on such ceramics, typically mythological scenes, battles, or themes of motherhood, depending on the specific vessel form.

For my piece, I chose to illustrate the myth of Apollo and Daphne. The story begins with the god Eros shooting Apollo with a golden arrow to incite love, and Daphne with a lead one to provoke rejection. Consumed by passion, Apollo chases Daphne, who desperately flees until she begs her father, the river god Peneus, to save her. He transforms her into a laurel tree to protect her.

The illustration captures the moment Daphne begins to metamorphose into the laurel while being pursued by Apollo. I drew inspiration from Gian Lorenzo Bernini’s famous sculpture Apollo and Daphne, adapting the posture and style to better align with ancient Greek vase aesthetics. In my interpretation, during the chase, Apollo accidentally tears a laurel branch from Daphne’s body, a fictional detail I created to add emotional depth and narrative tension.

I chose an amphora as the type of ceramic vessel for this artwork. Amphorae were commonly used for storing food and items such as laurel leaves, which connects thematically to the myth and adds contextual logic to the choice of form.

For the final poster design, I studied existing promotional materials from the MAN to replicate their visual identity. The poster includes the illustrated vase, the museum’s logo, the Spanish Ministry of Culture seal (as commonly seen in real promotional materials), a photograph of the museum’s Greek ceramics section, a title, teaser text, exhibition dates, and various graphic elements like gradients and transparencies to enhance the overall visual sophistication.
