DON'T

SMOKE

For this project in the Psychology of Perception course, we explored visual irony and social criticism by designing a poster that critiques excessive consumption, particularly smoking. The central concept contrasts the symbolism of a pencil, representing creativity and freedom, with that of a cigarette, which is associated with addiction and harm. The poster features a person smoking a pencil, subtly suggesting that the act of smoking could instead be a creative, harmless action. However, juxtaposed against this is the larger-than-life image of cigarette packs with skull illustrations on them, symbolizing the grave dangers of smoking.

The figure in the poster is turned away from the oversized cigarette packs, implying that the individual is ignoring the consequences of their actions. This deliberate positioning reinforces the message of avoidance and denial about the harmful effects of smoking. The exaggerated size of the cigarette packs further dramatizes the problem, suggesting that the longer one continues with harmful habits, the more the issue grows out of control.

Additionally, the cigarette packs are altered to include the phrase "Malfario" (bad luck), replacing the usual brand name and adding a sense of inevitability. The skull, a well-known symbol of death, amplifies the theme of mortality that comes with smoking. By replacing "cigarettes" with "pencils" on the packaging, the poster draws a direct connection between addiction and the loss of potential, underscoring how a seemingly small vice can lead to significant, irreversible consequences.

This piece of visual irony invites the viewer to reconsider their behavior, highlighting the dangers of addiction while using the pencil as a metaphor for creative freedom and personal choice.
